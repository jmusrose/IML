Abstract
Multimodal learning, which has been given great significance re-
cently, may face the challenge of the imbalanced multimodal phe-
nomenon, which leads to the insufficient optimization of both
multimodal and unimodal objectives. The core problem lies in
the optimization conflicts between the above optimization objec-
tives, resulting in the diverse updating directions and strengths
that cause antagonism between them. In this paper, we mathe-
matically analyze the optimization processes of imbalanced mul-
timodal learning in the hyperspaces from a novel geometric per-
spective. Additionally, based on our theoretical analysis, we de-
fined the volumes of the gradients constructed parallel polyhedron
in the hyperspace to quantify the misalignment between the op-
timization objectives. Subsequently, we proposed the Geometric
Gradient Divergence Modulation (GGDM), which leverages the
volumes of gradient polyhedron to perform gradient modulation,
encouraging alignment among gradients and promoting a syn-
ergistic optimization effect. Lastly, we evaluate our GGDM on
five widely used multimodal benchmarks, where RGB image, op-
tical flow, text, image, video and audio are involved. Our method
achieved state-of-the-art performance compared to other imbal-
anced multimodal learning methods. Our code is available at: https:
//github.com/ConstantineWayne/GGDM.

1 Introduction
Multimodal learning (MML) [2, 18, 29] integrates multiple signals
from heterogeneous modalities, which shows outstanding perfor-
mances compared to unimodal learning in various downstream
multimedia tasks, such as cross-modal retrieval [5, 42], multimedia
content understanding [23, 25], and affective computing [17, 24].
Recent researches [8, 13, 31, 38, 41, 44] have revealed the potential
risks of imbalanced multimodal learning, a phenomenon in which
one particular modality with richer semantic information will dom-
inate the whole optimization process, leading to the insufficiently
unleashing the potential of multimodal models.
Many researchers have tackled the challenge of Imbalanced Mul-
timodal Learning (IML). Previous works [13, 22, 28, 31] primarily
follow joint training paradigms with a single multimodal optimiza-
tion objective to balance learning across modalities. However, Wei
et al. [40] argue that previous IML methods focusing solely on uni-
modal perspectives overlook the fact that unimodal optimization
objectives can actually support multimodal learning by mitigating
competition between modalities. Building on this insight, methods
[12, 43] that effectively integrate both unimodal and multimodal

compete—have demonstrated superior performance compared to
those relying solely on unimodal optimization. Although they have
acquired promising results, we observe that these works [19, 31, 38]
predominantly characterize multimodal gradient interactions in
two-dimensional spaces, which implicitly assumes that gradients
can be fully described by their pairwise angular relationships in
low-dimensional space. However, this simplification overlooks the
fact that gradients from joint optimization objectives can unevenly
influence the updates of unimodal encoders, thereby indirectly alter-
ing their individual optimization trajectories. As a result, modeling
such interactions in a simple two-dimensional space fails to capture
the high-dimensional, intricate nature of multimodal optimization.
Based on this insight, we propose a hypothesis: What if we explore
these interactions in a higher-dimensional gradient space?
As illustrated in Fig. 1(a), we use a three-dimensional space as an
example to extend the traditional two-dimensional gradient space.
In this hyperspace, each gradient vector simultaneously captures
its magnitude (length), direction, and interactions (via angles) with
other objectives. Compared to conventional two-dimensional ap-
proaches, this higher-dimensional geometric structure enables a
more intuitive and comprehensive visualization of synergistic and
antagonistic effects among updating gradients. For example, large
angles between a unimodal objective and components of the mul-
timodal objective may indicate potential conflicts, while similar
effects can be observed among the components within the multi-
modal objective itself. Moreover, disparities in gradient magnitudes
can lead to imbalanced learning, where certain modalities dominate
the optimization process. Finally, the convex polyhedron spanned
by the gradient vectors captures all possible linear combinations
of these gradients, providing a geometric framework for under-
standing and modulating the space of feasible parameter updates.
Since the interactions between different optimization objectives
are more clearly manifested in high-dimensional gradient spaces,
a natural question arises: How can we quantitatively measure the
misalignment and imbalance among modalities in this space?
Motivated by this question, we propose using the volume of the
convex polyhedron formed by the updating gradients as a geometric
proxy for optimization imbalance. As shown in Fig. 1(b), we empiri-
cally observe that the polyhedron volume tends to decrease during
training while the corresponding modality accuracy increases. This
suggests a potential correlation between gradient volume and mul-
timodal model performance, where smaller volumes may indicate
better alignment and more balanced updates. To further validate
this hypothesis, we not only provide a theoretical analysis but also
conduct comprehensive empirical studies to reveal the relationship.
In this paper, we first provide a novel geometric perspective to
reveal the property of multimodal optimization and the interac-
tions between different optimization objectives in the hyperspace.
Furtherly, we theoretically explain the imbalanced multimodal phe-
nomenon using the gradients formulated parallel polyhedron and
confirm that the key to mitigate the imbalanced multimodal learn-
ing is to shrink the polyhedron’s volumes. Additionally, we im-
plement the gradient modulation technique, namely Geometric
Gradient Divergence Modulation (GGDM), to align the multimodal
and unimodal optimization objectives to reduce antagonism and
exert synergistic effects between them. Finally, we evaluate GGDM
on five multimodal datasets comprised RGB images, optical flow,
text, audio, and video, and our method reaches state-of-the-art
performance on all of the datasets.
In conclusion, our contribution can be summarized in threefold:
(1) We provide a theoretical analysis of the interactions between
multimodal and unimodal optimization objectives from a geometric
perspective in high-dimensional spaces. (2) We introduce a geomet-
ric metric to quantify the gradient divergence during multimodal
learning: the volume of the convex polyhedron spanned by dif-
ferent gradients, which quantifies the degree of imbalance during
optimization. (3) We incorporate gradient polyhedron volumes
into a gradient modulation strategy to reduce conflicts between
optimization objectives and mitigate modality imbalance.

Metric Geometry for Machine Learning. The learned repre-
sentations in machine learning models typically reside in high-
dimensional, non-uniform geometric structures, where traditional
metrics (e.g., Euclidean distance) often fail to capture the true rela-
tionships among data distributions. To better interpret these com-
plex structures, a growing body of work has explored the use of
metric geometry by embedding representations into structured geo-
metric spaces. For instance, Wasserstein spaces provide a principled
way to model the geometry of posterior distributions using Wasser-
stein distance, enabling more expressive comparisons between prob-
ability measures [15, 34]. Similarly, Riemannian manifolds allow
high-dimensional data to be projected onto lower-dimensional non-
linear manifolds, where geometric properties—such as angles and
curvature—can be exploited to enhance the quality of feature embed-
dings [6, 32, 35]. Furthermore, compared to flat Euclidean spaces,
hyperbolic spaces—with their negative curvature—have demon-
strated superior representational power, especially for hierarchical
or imbalanced structures [14, 16, 30, 39]. Motivated by these in-
sights, we adopt the lens of metric geometry in hyperspaces to
better understand the “imbalance in multimodal learning”.

4 Experiments
4.1 Datasets and Implementation Details
Datasets. We leveraged our methods on five multimodal datasets
to demonstrate its effectiveness, i.e., CREMA-D [4], AVE [36], Ki-
neticsSounds [1], Twitter2017 [45], and UCF101 [33]. The former
three datasets are audio-visual datasets. Specifically, CREMA-D
contains 7,442 video clips; AVE consists of 4,143 10-second videos;
Kinetics Sounds [26] encompasses 19,000 video clips categorized in
31 categories. Moreover, Twitter2017, a dataset consisting of image
and text modalities, has three emotion labels: positive, negative, and
neutral. Lastly, the UCF101 [33] dataset, comprising 13,320 videos,
with 9,537 for training and 3,783 for testing, aims to predict human
motions using different modalities.
Implementation Details. In our experiments, we followed the for-
mer research on imbalanced multimodal learning using ResNet18
[20] as the foundational architecture to process both visual and
acoustic modalities. Concretely, for audio-visual datasets, the acous-
tic modality is transformed into 257 × 1004 spectrograms, and we
randomly sample multiple frames from 10-frame video clips. On
the other hand, for Twitter2017, the text-image dataset, we incor-
porate ResNet50 for image feature extraction and BERT [7] for text
processing. While for the modalities in UCF101, we also leveraged
ResNet18 to extract the features from the raw data. Optimizers of
audio-visual datasets are stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with
momentum of 0.9 and weight decay parameter of 1𝑒−1, and we set
the initial learning rate as 1𝑒−2 with batch size of 64, progressively
reducing it by a factor of ten upon observing a plateau in loss re-
duction. Finally, for the UCF101 dataset, we implemented tv-l1 to
extract Optical Flow from the RGB images. And for the text-image
dataset, we implement Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1𝑒−4.
All the datasets are trained with 150 epochs.

5 Conclusion
In conclusion, we first provided a novel geometric perspective
to reveal the optimization processes in multimodal learning and
imbalanced multimodal phenomenon. With solid mathematical de-
duction, we successfully explained the interactions of unimodal and
multimodal optimization objectives in the hyperspaces, in which
they collectively formulate the parallel polyhedron. Moreover, we
introduced the volume of the constructed polyhedron to reflect
the disparity degree between those two optimization objectives.
And used it to modulate the unimodal and multimodal gradients to
force them to align to avoid the imbalance phenomenon. At last,
we tested our method on five widely used multimodal datasets to
exhibit the effectiveness and robustness of our proposed method
and reached state-of-the-art performance. For future work, we shall
extend our method to scenarios close to real-world applications.
